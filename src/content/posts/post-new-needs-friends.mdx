Most early stage founders are not bad at pitching. They are struggling to translate something that does not yet have a language. That difference matters more than most venture investors like to admit.

Everyone in venture capital claims to be looking for something new. But when something is genuinely new, conceptually new and not yet legible inside existing categories, it tends to arrive awkwardly. It shows up half formed. It resists clean explanations. Sometimes even the founder does not fully understand it yet.

That is usually where the process breaks.

### When novelty does not fit the process

Founders building at the edge often describe the same experience. What they are working on feels out there, even to them. There are no established metaphors. The market language feels wrong. The pitch deck sounds thinner than the idea itself.

One founder put it simply:

> The stuff that I’m doing is so new and so out there. I barely understand it myself, let alone explain it to other people.

That is not confusion. It is proximity. When you are inside something genuinely new, you are too close to it to see a clean narrative from the outside.

Most VC processes are optimized for the opposite condition. Clear stories.
Familiar frames. Ideas that already know how to introduce themselves. When
founders cannot provide that, they are often asked to come back later, once the
story is clearer.

But for truly new ideas, the story is often the hardest unsolved problem.

### In defense of the new

There is a line spoken by Anton Ego in Ratatouille that feels unusually relevant to early-stage investing:

> The world is often unkind to new talent, new creations. The new needs friends.

> — Anton Ego, Ratatouille

In that moment, Ego is reflecting on the role of the critic. At their best, critics are not destroyers. They are defenders of the new, especially when it arrives imperfectly and without consensus.

That role maps uncomfortably well onto venture capital.

/>

Many investors behave like critics in the shallow sense. They wait for validation. They look for signals from other investors. They follow motion instead of doing the work of judgment.

You hear it openly acknowledged. Some VCs see others invest and then they invest too. No deep thinking required. Just confirmation.

> 'That’s what they do,' as one founder immediately responded when this came up.

But that is not defending the new. That is avoiding responsibility for understanding it.

Defending the new means being willing to sit with ideas that are incomplete, difficult to communicate, and uncomfortable to evaluate. It means engaging before consensus forms, not after.

### Time is the real signal

What founders often respond to most is not capital. It is time.

Time spent reading closely.
Time spent doing real analysis instead of pattern matching.
Time spent asking questions that clarify rather than interrogate.

One founder described the difference this way:

> It feels like you actually understand what we’re doing. More than anyone else we’ve talked to.

That reaction did not come from agreement or commitment. It came from engagement. From treating the conversation as a shared attempt to understand something new, rather than a test the founder had to pass.

At the earliest stages, time is not just courtesy. It is a signal of seriousness.

### Storytelling is not a solo task

Founders are constantly told to “tell a better story,” as if storytelling is something they should solve alone.

For genuinely new technology, storytelling is almost always a collaborative problem.

The founder has the raw material. The lived experience. The intuition. The technical depth. But they are often too embedded to see which parts matter most to someone outside the system.

<MarkdownPostImage

A good early stage investor does not just ask for a clean story. They help find it.

That can mean reframing the idea in different ways. Stress testing explanations together. Separating what is essential from what is incidental.

Not as a favor, but as part of the work. You cannot understand a new idea without helping shape the language around it.

### What early stage venture actually requires

At the earliest stages, the most important investor trait is not speed, brand, or access.

It is intellectual empathy.

The ability to say, honestly, “I do not fully understand this yet, but I am willing to think with you until I do.”

That posture is rare. It is also disproportionately valuable.

Because the biggest breakthroughs do not arrive fully explained. They arrive strange, fragile, and easy to dismiss.

Which is exactly why the new needs friends.

At PWV, that idea is simple and explicit. Ideas start with founders. Founders start with PWV.

Not because PWV has answers early, but because early is when ideas most need someone willing to sit with confusion, engage deeply, and help make sense of what is still forming.
